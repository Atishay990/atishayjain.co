A Philosophical Exploration

Free Will vs Destiny

On astrology, cosmic clocks, and the question humanity has never been able to answer

Chapter I

The Wrong Question

Everyone asks the same thing about astrology: Is it real?

They argue about it at dinner tables. They mock it on Twitter. They secretly check their horoscope at 2 AM. And the debate always goes the same way. One side says "the planets control your fate," the other side says "it's all nonsense," and both leave the conversation exactly where they started.

I used to be in the "nonsense" camp. The physics is settled, after all. Saturn is a ball of hydrogen and helium. If gas giants influenced human behaviour, holding a helium balloon should change your personality. It doesn't. The gravitational pull of the doctor who delivered you was stronger than the pull of Mars. Electromagnetic fields from planets don't reach you in any measurable way. Science closed this case decades ago.

But then a thought hit me. One that made me realise the entire debate was asking the wrong question.

What if the planets don't cause anything, but they tell time?

Chapter II

The Alien and the Wall Clock

Imagine an alien lands in your living room. He has never been to Earth. He knows nothing about our sun, our rotation, our days and nights. He just sees you, a strange creature sitting on a couch, surrounded by odd objects.

He notices a device on your wall. A circle with two pointed needles and twelve numbers. He watches it for a while. The needles move. Slowly, predictably.

Then he notices something strange. Every time the small needle points to 8 and the big needle points to 12, the light outside disappears. The sky turns dark. It happens again and again. Like clockwork. Literally.

The Alien's Conclusion

"These needles are powerful. When the small one strikes 8 and the big one strikes 12, they fight. The angle they create, roughly 120 degrees, unleashes a war between the two forces. And this war kills the light. The needles cause the darkness."

The alien writes this down in his journal as a law of Earth physics. He is wrong about why it happens. But he is right that it happens. Every single time.

You and I know the truth. The clock doesn't cause the sunset. The sun sets because the Earth rotates. But humans built the clock to match that rotation. We calibrated our needles to align with the cycles already happening. The clock is a mirror, not a machine.

Now. Hold that thought and look up.

Chapter III

The Clock Nobody Built

Here is a question nobody asks: does the universe have a clock?

We have clocks for Earth. Every country has its own time zone, its own way of slicing the day. But for the solar system? For the cosmos? There's no master clock hanging on the wall of space.

And yet we need one. If you wanted to build a universal clock, what would you use? You'd need things that are predictable, that move in fixed patterns, that can be quantified and projected forward and backward in time. You'd need the cosmic equivalent of needles on a dial.

You'd need planets.

Planets move in fixed orbits. Their positions can be calculated with extraordinary precision, thousands of years into the past, thousands into the future. They are the most reliable ticking mechanism in the known universe. Saturn takes 29.5 years to complete one orbit. Jupiter takes 12. Mars takes 2. Each one is a needle, sweeping around a cosmic dial at its own pace.

The Realisation

What if the ancient astrologers weren't physicists who got the mechanism wrong, but engineers who built a clock using the only materials available? The planets don't cause events on Earth. They mark them. They are the needles. They are not the engine.

When someone says "Saturn moved into Aries and war broke out," perhaps what they really mean is: when the small needle hit 8, it got dark outside. Both statements are true. Both events happened. But one did not cause the other. They are two expressions of the same underlying cycle, tracked by different instruments.

Chapter IV

The Broken Dial

Now. Before you run to Twitter and declare that you've proven astrology works, let me stop you. Because there's a fatal flaw in this beautiful theory, and honesty demands we look at it.

Your wall clock works because someone calibrated it. An engineer connected the needle's movement to Earth's rotation with mathematical precision. When the clock says 8:00 PM, it is 8:00 PM. Every time. The error rate is nearly zero.

The "cosmic clock"? Nobody calibrated it.

Saturn has been in Aries many times in recorded history. Sometimes wars happened. Sometimes there was peace. Sometimes nothing notable happened at all. If Saturn-in-Aries is supposed to be the "8 PM" of global conflict, it's a clock that points to 8 PM during sunrise, noon, and midnight at random.

You would throw that clock in the trash.

The Hard Truth

A clock that's right sometimes and wrong sometimes is not a clock. It's a decoration. And a prediction system that works occasionally and fails frequently is not a system. It's a story we tell ourselves between the misses.

Researchers have tested this. In 1985, a study published in Nature gave top astrologers natal charts and asked them to match personalities. They performed at the rate of random chance. In 2003, scientists tracked 2,000 "time twins", people born minutes apart in the same hospital, over decades. They had completely different lives, personalities, and outcomes. Same cosmic clock. Different movies.

If the clock exists, we haven't cracked the code. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

Chapter V

The Deeper Question

But here's what kept nagging at me even after the astrology debate collapsed. If the clock is broken, if we can't read the future from the planets, there's still a question underneath that refuses to go away:

Is there a future to be read at all? Or are we writing it as we go?

This is the free will question. The oldest, heaviest question humanity has ever carried. And it matters far more than astrology, because your answer to it changes how you live.

Physics gives us two answers that directly contradict each other.

Einstein's universe says yes, the future is already written. In his model, time is a dimension, like length or width. The past, present, and future all exist simultaneously, like a loaf of bread. You are simply a consciousness moving through a slice, experiencing one frame at a time. You feel like you're making choices. But the next frame already exists. You just haven't gotten there yet.

Quantum mechanics says no, the future is being generated in real-time. At the subatomic level, particles don't have fixed paths. They exist in clouds of probability. An electron isn't "here" or "there" until something observes it. If the building blocks of reality are fundamentally uncertain, then the future can't be fully written. There are blank pages ahead.

And neuroscience makes it even worse. In the 1980s, researchers found that the brain begins preparing for a decision before you consciously feel like you've made it. The hand starts moving before the "you" inside decides to move it. Which raises a terrifying possibility: maybe your conscious mind isn't the driver. Maybe it's just the narrator, telling you a story about choices that were already made by your neurons.

So science is deadlocked. Destiny, chance, or the illusion of both. Take your pick.

Chapter VI

The Battlefield Answer

Thousands of years before Einstein, before quantum physics, before brain scanners, a warrior stood on a battlefield and asked the same question.

Arjuna, the greatest archer of his age, froze. Not because he was afraid of dying. Because he was afraid of choosing. The people on the other side of the battlefield were his family, his teachers, his blood. If the universe was just, if there was a cosmic order, how could this war be part of it? And if the outcome was already decided by fate, why should he lift his bow at all?

Krishna's answer, delivered in the middle of a battlefield with two armies waiting, is the most elegant solution to the free will problem ever articulated. It has a name: Nishkam Karma, selfless action without attachment to the fruit. And it doesn't require a physics degree to understand.

Nishkam Karma — The Gita's Answer

"Karmanye vadhikaraste ma phaleshu kadachana."

You have the right to action alone, never to its fruits. This is the heart of Nishkam Karma, the doctrine of desireless action.

Everything is already destined. The war will happen. The outcome is written. But you, Arjuna, are a vessel. You are not here to control the result. You are here because fighting is your nature.

You are a Kshatriya. A warrior. Your Svadharma, your innate nature, is to fight. Not because fighting will change the outcome. But because a warrior who does not fight is denying what he is. The river does not choose to flow. It flows because it is a river. You do not choose to fight. You fight because you are Arjuna.

Act, but release the outcome. The fruit of action is not yours to hold.

Read that again slowly. Nishkam Karma doesn't say "do nothing because everything is destined." It says the opposite. Do everything, but do it without clinging to the result. Destiny makes the outcome irrelevant. Your nature is the engine. The result is scenery. The action, performed without desire for reward, performed simply because it is your dharma to perform it, that is the purest form of being alive.

Two Kinds of Action

Sakam Karma, action performed with attachment to the result. I work so I can get rich. I love so I can be loved back. I create so I can be praised. The moment the reward disappears, the action collapses. The puppet's strings are cut.

Nishkam Karma, action performed without attachment to the result. I work because work is my nature. I love because love is what I am. I create because creation is the only thing that makes the hours feel honest. The reward is the action itself. Cut whatever strings you want. The body keeps moving.

Chapter VII

The Destiny Test

Here is where the free will debate stops being philosophy and starts being a mirror held up to your face.

Imagine someone told you, with absolute, verified certainty, that the thing you're working on right now is going to fail. The business, the book, the art, the relationship. Guaranteed failure. It's written in the code of the universe. There is no version of the timeline where it succeeds.

What do you do?

If you stop, if you put down the tools and walk away, then you were never doing it for the work. You were doing it for the reward. The outcome was the only thing pulling you forward, and without it, there's no reason to move.

But if you keep going, if something inside you says "I don't care, I can't stop, this is what I do", then you've found your Svadharma. Your nature. The thing you were built for. And in that moment, the question of free will vs destiny stops mattering entirely.

The builder who keeps building after being told the building will fall, that is someone who has answered the free will question with their hands, not their head.

And this is the Gita's most radical insight. People fear that if you tell the world "everything is destined," everyone will become lazy. They'll sit on the couch and say "what's the point?" But Krishna saw through that fear. He knew that destiny acts as a filter. If telling someone the truth makes them stop, that was their nature too. The lazy response was also part of their code. You didn't create the laziness. You revealed it.

Destiny doesn't make you passive. It sorts you. It separates the people who work for the prize from the people who work because they can't not work. It separates the performers from the players.

Chapter VIII

So, Free Will or Destiny?

So let me put it all together.

Astrology as physics? Dead. The planets don't shoot lasers at your love life. Saturn doesn't cause wars. Mercury doesn't break your Wi-Fi. Science closed that door and welded it shut.

Astrology as a cosmic clock? Beautiful theory, unproven. The idea that planets are needles on a universal dial, marking cycles rather than causing them, is elegant and worth thinking about. But the clock hasn't been calibrated. It points to 8 PM at random hours. Until it's accurate, it's decoration, not navigation.

Free will? Probably an illusion, or at best a very thin sliver of agency operating inside a vast ocean of things you didn't choose. Your genes, your upbringing, the century you were born in, the neurons firing before you "decide" to fire them.

Destiny? Possibly real. Einstein's physics says the future already exists. Your brain decides before "you" do. The block universe just sits there, fully formed, and you are a consciousness sliding through a pre-cut groove.

But here's the thing that none of the physicists, philosophers, or neuroscientists have been able to explain away: it doesn't matter.

Whether the movie is pre-recorded or filming live, you still have to act in it. Whether the chess game was decided at move one or is being played in real-time, you still have to move the pieces. Whether the universe is a block of ice or a flowing river, you are still wet.

The Final Position

Free will and destiny might not be opposites. They might be the same thing wearing different masks.

If everything is destined, then your nature, the thing you can't stop doing, the work you'd do for free, the fight you'd pick even when you're losing, was also destined. Your free will is the destiny. The river doesn't choose to flow. But it flows with its full force anyway. Is that freedom or fate? Maybe the question was always broken. Maybe the answer was always: both.

The clock ticks. The planets orbit. The needles point to a number you can't read. And somewhere down here, on a small rock in an unremarkable corner of an unfathomable universe, a builder wakes up and builds. Not because the stars said so. Not because free will compelled it. But because that's what builders do, whether the script was written or not.

Written somewhere between free will and fate, unsure which one held the pen.
